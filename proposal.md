Policy Proposal: Supporting Entry-Level Workers in Australia's AI-Driven Labour Market

To: Deputy Secretary, Department of Employment and Workplace Relations  
From: Hao Du 
Date: 2026/5/2  
Subject: Policy response to AI-driven changes in the Australian labour market  

Executive Summary

Artificial intelligence is changing the Australian labour market by reshaping tasks, skills, wages, and employment opportunities. While AI can improve productivity and create new forms of work, it may also reduce demand for some routine and entry-level jobs. This is especially important for young workers, graduates, administrative workers, and people in roles involving repetitive digital tasks. This proposal recommends that the Australian Government introduce an AI labour-market adjustment package that combines subsidised AI skills training, employer AI impact assessments, and support for small and medium-sized businesses to adopt AI in ways that complement workers rather than replace them.

1. Problem Definition and Context

Artificial intelligence is increasingly being used in workplaces to automate tasks such as writing, data entry, customer service, document review, scheduling, and basic analysis. These tools do not always replace entire occupations, but they can replace or change specific tasks within jobs. This means that workers in routine office-based roles may face greater pressure to adapt.

The policy problem is that the benefits and risks of AI are unlikely to be evenly distributed. Workers with strong digital skills may become more productive and earn higher wages, while workers with lower AI literacy may face reduced job opportunities. Entry-level workers are particularly vulnerable because many junior roles involve tasks that AI can now perform quickly, such as drafting documents, summarising information, or preparing simple reports.

2. Analysis of AI Impacts on Labour Markets

AI affects labour markets through several channels. First, it changes the task content of jobs. Rather than removing all jobs, AI often automates parts of jobs. For example, administrative workers may still be needed, but some routine writing, record management, and customer communication tasks may be automated.

Second, AI may affect employment. Some firms may reduce hiring for entry-level roles if AI tools allow existing workers to complete more tasks. However, AI may also create demand for new skills, such as prompt writing, AI supervision, data analysis, and digital workflow management.

Third, AI can affect wages. Workers who can use AI effectively may become more productive and may receive higher wages. However, workers whose tasks are easier to automate may experience weaker bargaining power or slower wage growth.

Fourth, AI may change industry structure. Large firms are more likely to have the resources to adopt AI quickly, while small and medium-sized businesses may struggle with cost, training, and technical knowledge. This could increase productivity gaps between firms.

3. Policy Options

| Policy Option | Description | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|
| Option 1: Subsidised AI skills training | Provide government-funded AI literacy and digital skills courses for at-risk workers | Improves worker adaptability and supports long-term employability | May be costly and requires strong course quality control |
| Option 2: Employer AI impact assessments | Require large employers to assess how AI adoption affects jobs, tasks, and worker rights | Improves transparency and reduces unfair labour-market impacts | May increase compliance costs for businesses |
| Option 3: SME AI adoption support | Support small and medium-sized businesses to adopt AI in worker-complementing ways | Helps smaller firms improve productivity and remain competitive | Could still encourage automation if not designed carefully |

4. Recommended Policy Package and Implementation Plan

The recommended approach is a combined policy package. The government should introduce subsidised AI skills training for workers in at-risk occupations, require large employers to conduct AI impact assessments, and provide support for small and medium-sized businesses that use AI to complement workers rather than replace them.

The Department of Employment and Workplace Relations should lead the policy, working with Jobs and Skills Australia, TAFE providers, universities, unions, and employer groups. The policy could begin with a 12-month pilot program in sectors such as administration, customer service, finance support, and professional services.

Implementation should include three stages. First, the government should identify occupations most exposed to AI-driven task change. Second, it should fund short AI literacy and digital skills courses for workers in these occupations. Third, it should require large employers using AI in workforce decisions to publish basic AI impact assessments.

The success of the policy should be measured through clear indicators, including the number of workers trained, course completion rates, employment outcomes after training, wage changes, and employer compliance with AI impact assessments.

5. Risks and Mitigation

One risk is that training programs may not match real workplace needs. This can be reduced by involving employers, unions, and education providers in course design. Another risk is that AI impact assessments may become a box-ticking exercise. To avoid this, the government should provide clear reporting standards and require regular review. A further risk is that businesses may use AI mainly to reduce labour costs. Government support should therefore prioritise AI adoption that improves productivity while maintaining worker participation.

Conclusion

AI will continue to reshape the Australian labour market. The policy challenge is not simply to stop automation, but to make sure workers can adapt and share in the productivity benefits. A combined package of AI skills training, employer transparency, and SME support would help Australia respond to AI in a balanced way. This approach supports productivity while also reducing the risk that young, entry-level, and routine-task workers are left behind.
